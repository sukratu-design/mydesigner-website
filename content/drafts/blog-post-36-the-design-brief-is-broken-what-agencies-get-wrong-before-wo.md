# The Design Brief Is Broken: What Agencies Get Wrong Before Work Even Starts

You have just received the first design deliverable. It is polished. It is well-crafted. It is also nothing like what you needed.

The revisions begin. Then the counter-revisions. Timelines shift. Frustration builds on both sides. By the time the project limps across the finish line, the original brief — a two-paragraph email dashed off three weeks ago — has been quietly forgotten.

This story plays out across agencies every day. And the cause is rarely the designer.

According to the [Project Management Institute](https://www.ascertra.com/blog/pmi-study-reveals-poor-communication-leads-to-project-failure-one-third-of-the-time), ineffective communications is the primary contributor to project failure one-third of the time — and negatively impacts project success in more than half of all cases. Companies risk $75 million of every $135 million project risk per $1 billion spent, directly attributable to poor communication. The design brief is where that communication either starts well or starts to unravel.

---

## The Brief Is Where Projects Are Won or Lost

Most agencies treat the brief as a formality — a box to tick before the real work begins. In practice, it is the opposite. The brief is the most important document in any design engagement. It defines what success looks like before anyone opens Figma.

When a brief is vague, designers are forced to make assumptions. They fill gaps with their own aesthetic instincts, industry conventions, and best guesses about your audience. Some of those guesses will be right. Many will not be. And [52% of projects already experience scope creep](https://www.pmi.org/-/media/pmi/documents/public/pdf/learning/thought-leadership/pulse/pulse-of-the-profession-2018.pdf) — uncontrolled changes that expand beyond the original scope, according to PMI's Pulse of the Profession. A weak brief is scope creep's primary enabler.

The brief is not just a communication tool. It is an alignment tool. Done properly, it means you and your designer are solving the same problem before a single pixel is touched.

## Five Things Most Briefs Get Wrong

### 1. Stating outputs instead of outcomes

"We need a landing page" is not a brief objective. It is a deliverable. The difference matters enormously.

Outputs describe what you want built. Outcomes describe what you want to happen. "We need a landing page that converts paid traffic into trial sign-ups, targeting senior marketers at mid-sized B2B companies" gives a designer something to optimise for. "We need a landing page" gives them a canvas with no direction.

Start every brief by asking: what does success look like for the business, not the design?

### 2. Audience descriptions that are too broad

"Our target audience is professionals aged 25–45" is nearly useless. Every designer will interpret it differently.

Good audience descriptions go further: what does this person care about, what does their working day look like, what objections do they have, and what would make them trust your brand? The more specific the audience definition, the more targeted the design decisions become. A focused audience gives designers something to design for.

### 3. No competitive context

Design does not exist in a vacuum. Your audience has already encountered your competitors, absorbed their visual language, and formed expectations. If your brief does not include a competitive landscape, you are asking your designer to navigate that context blind.

Even a brief paragraph — three competitors, what they do visually, and why you want to be different — gives a designer a map. Without it, they will either unconsciously mirror conventions in your sector or deliberately break them for the wrong reasons.

### 4. Missing constraints and non-negotiables

What you do not want is as important as what you do. Most briefs list desires. Very few list constraints.

Some examples of the constraints agencies forget to include: brand colours that are fixed vs. flexible, typefaces already in use elsewhere, content that must appear above the fold, legal requirements for disclaimers, platforms or accessibility standards the design must meet.

Designers who are not told these things will often find out mid-project — or worse, after delivery. Documenting constraints upfront saves everyone time and prevents the particular frustration of receiving a beautiful design you cannot use.

### 5. No definition of done

What does a successful deliverable actually look like? How will you know when the project is complete? Without answers to these questions, a project can stretch indefinitely.

A definition of done does not have to be complicated. It might be: three responsive screen sizes, delivered as a Figma file with named components, reviewed by the marketing lead, with one round of revisions included. That single sentence eliminates most of the ambiguity that causes projects to drag.

## What a Great Brief Actually Contains

A brief that sets a project up to succeed typically covers six things:

**The problem, not the solution.** Describe the business or user problem you are trying to solve. Let the designer determine how to solve it visually.

**A specific audience.** Go beyond demographics. Describe the person, their context, and what would earn their trust or attention.

**The success metric.** What does good look like? More sign-ups, stronger brand recall, faster time-to-information — pick a metric and state it.

**Competitive context.** Three to five competitors, a note on their visual approach, and where you want to sit in relation to them.

**Constraints and non-negotiables.** Brand rules, technical requirements, legal considerations, anything fixed.

**Timeline and decision-making.** Who approves work, how many revision rounds are included, and what the key dates are.

This is not a template to fill in mechanically. It is a checklist for having the right conversation before work begins.

## The Brief Is a Conversation, Not a Contract

The best briefs are not written in isolation. They are produced through dialogue.

[High-performing organisations create formal communication plans for nearly twice as many projects](https://www.ascertra.com/blog/pmi-study-reveals-poor-communication-leads-to-project-failure-one-third-of-the-time) as lower-performing counterparts, according to PMI research. The brief is the first instalment of that communication plan.

Before work begins, share a draft brief with your designer and ask them to push back on it. A good designer will spot gaps you have missed, ask questions that surface unstated assumptions, and tell you if the brief contains conflicting objectives. That conversation — before anything is designed — is worth more than any number of revision rounds after the fact.

Think of it less as a specification and more as a shared understanding. The brief should leave both parties feeling aligned, not contracted.

## A Better Brief Means Better Design

When agencies come to [mydesigner.gg](https://www.mydesigner.gg) to find design talent, the quality of the brief determines the quality of the engagement. A vague brief limits what even an exceptional designer can deliver. A clear, specific brief unlocks it.

The investment in a good brief is small — an hour of structured thinking before the project starts. The return is a designer who can hit the target on the first pass, a project that stays within scope, and a working relationship built on clarity rather than guesswork.

Before your next project, spend that hour on the brief. The rest of the project will be easier for it.
